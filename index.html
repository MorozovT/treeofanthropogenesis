Purgatorius

Believed to be the earliest example of a primate or a proto-primate, dating to as old as 66 million years ago. Omnivorous, thus had to keep in mind all the possible food sources, which led to intellectual development. The species jumped on trees (heel and talus bones reveal it), developing its vestibular apparatus. Relied chiefly on their vision instead of the smell.

Proconsul

The gibbons and great apes, including humans, are held in evolutionary biology to share a common ancestral lineage, which may have included Proconsul. Same length of legs and arms. However, a human-like jaw and increased brain indicate its belonging to the anthropoid apes.

Nakalipithecus nakayamai

The last common ancestor of gorillas, chimpanzees, and hominids. Since the only jaw was found, nothing could be noted about its way of movement.

The early Australopithecines
Sahelanthropus tchadensis

Is believed to be the ancestor of hominids. The position of the foramen magnum indicates that the Sahelanthropus probably was bipedal. It is placed a bit forward, still having some quadrupedal traits.

The early Australopithecines
Orrorin tugenensis

The remains are probably of at least 5 specimens. According to the leg and finger bones, the creatures were able both to walk on two legs and climb on trees.

The early Australopithecines
Ardipithecus kadabba

A. kadabba is known from 19 specimens that reveal elements of the teeth, jaw, feet, and hands and arms.The jaw structure from the remains is primitive, like the Orrorins’ and Sahelanthropus’ ones.

The early Australopithecines
Ardipithecus ramidus

The body structure equally combines human and ape traits. A.ramidus has adaptations for both bipedality and arboreality. Cutting of the skull has caused the opportunity for vocal centers development, which affected society’s complexity.

The early Australopithecines
Australopithecus anamensis

The finger remains revealed that despite the well-developed bipedalism, a. anamensis walked on all fours with the help of their bent fingers. The body structure equally combines human and ape traits.

Gracial Austrolapithecus
Australopithecus afarensis

Lucy is the most famous exponent of the species.The specimens had small brains, which was a surprising fact as bipedalism was associated with brain development. The long hands reveal the fact that despite bipedalism, a.afarensis mostly lived on trees.

Gracial Austrolapithecus
Australopithecus garhi

A. garhi is the first pre-Homo hominid postulated to have manufactured tools—using them in butchering—and may be counted among a growing body of evidence for pre-Homo stone tool industries.

Gracial Austrolapithecus
Australopithecus africanus

In comparison with A. afarensis, had a more ape-like skeleton, but a bigger skull.

Massive Austrolapithecus
Paranthropus boisei

Robust australopithecines are characterized by heavily built skulls capable of producing high stresses and bite forces and some of the largest molars with the thickest enamel of any known ape.

Massive Austrolapithecus
Paranthropus robustus

P. robustus may have used bones as tools to extract and process food. It is unclear if P. robustus lived in a harem society like gorillas or a multi-male society like baboons. P. robustus society may have been patrilocal, with adult females more likely to leave the group than males.

Early Homo
Homo rudolfensis

The earliest Homo. Because H. rudolfensis coexisted with several other hominins, it is debated what specimens can be confidently assigned to this species beyond the skull KNM-ER 1470 and other partial skull aspects. Early Homo species exhibit marked brain growth compared to Australopithecus predecessors, which is typically explained as a change in diet with a calorie-rich food source, namely meat.

Early Homo
Homo georgicus

The earliest non-african hominids. Anatomically, the H. Georgicus exhibited a mosaic of traits, simultaneously having more progressive features of H.erectus and modern humans, and more archaic ones of earlier Homo and Australopithecus.The length and modern morphology of their legs were adapted to long-range walking and running, but their arms were likely more similar to the arms of Australopithecus than to later hominins.

Early Homo
Homo habilis

H.habilis is believed to be the first hominid species who started to use stone tools.The species differed from his ancestors by significantly increased brains with more progressive structure and more functional palm. Moreover, new pelvis structure enabled better bipedalism and birth of children with bigger skulls. The skull's form indicates Broca’s area presence, which is the speech center.

Archantrops
Homo ergaster

The body structure is similar to the h.habilis's, but the skeleton became lighter, and the frontal lobe and Broca’s area have increased. In fact, h.ergaster could have the rudiments of speech. Probably, these hominids began to use fire.

Archantrops
African homo erectus

H. erectus was the first human ancestor to spread throughout the Old World, having a distribution in Eurasia extending from the Iberian Peninsula to Java. The species had a humanlike gait and body proportions, and was the first human species to have exhibited a flat face, prominent nose, and possibly sparse body hair coverage.It is thought to have been the earliest human ancestor capable of using fire, hunting and gathering in coordinated groups, caring for injured or sick group members, seafaring, and possibly art-making. H. erectus was anatomically capable of speech, though it is postulated they communicated using some proto-language.

Archantrops
Homo erectus pekinensis

Asian of of homo erectus. Many remains were found in Zhoukoudian cave. The specimens in different strata differ significantly- the higher the remains, the more progressive are. Thus, a local evolution occurred there.

Archantrops
Pitecanthropus

H.erectus population that migrated to Java island, where due to ocean level change became isolated.

Archantrops
Solo man

H.erectus subspecies that formed during the local evolution of Java. Because of the tools found with the fossils and many of their more gracile anatomical features, Solo Man was first classified as a subspecies of Homo sapiens.

Archantrops
Homo floresiensis

The species was called ‘hobbit’ for its small height (around one meter). Despite its enormously small brains (around 400 cubic cm, which is smaller than a chimpanzee’s one), h.floresiensis was familiar with stone tools production.Multiple theories are considered about the species. One states that the hominids were not a seperate species, but a group of homo sapiens with some pathology.

Paleantrops
Homo antecessor

The skulls of this species share features with both H. erectus and modern humans; its brain was nearly as large as that of modern humans.

Paleantrops
European homo heidelbergensis

The species which developed many essential things, such as composite weapons, settlements, religion, art, and ubiquitous usage of fire.

Paleantrops
Neanderthals

The best researched homo species. Were massiver and more muscular because it helped to keep warmth in the ice age. Neanderthals had mostly meat in their diet, including big game. Fire usage was familiar for them, however, they did not always cook their food. Neanderthals’ culture was more primitive than European early modern humans’ one: they performed less rituals, did not have art and long-range weapons.

Paleantrops
Homo rhodesiensis

The species distinguishes by the combination of archaic and modern traits: the forehead is massive, more ape-like, whereas the temporal lobe of the brain is similar to ours.

Paleantrops
Homo helmei

As opposed to Neanderthals, h.helmei had mental eminence which connects muscles required for speech. The species produced weapon tips, which means that the hominid possibly used stabbing and throwing weapons.

Paleantrops
Denisovans

The DNA analysis revealed the fact that denisovans experienced multiple hybridizations- the species shares many similar genes with homo neanderthalensis, and less with homo sapiens. However, there is 1% of archaic genes which Neanderthals do not have. Thus, denisovans possibly had contacts with other ‘the fourth’ homo species.
